      Kinetics : Kinetics is the branch of mechanics that deals with a body in motion.

      Kinematics : The term 'Kinematics' is derived from the Greek word 'kinema' meaning motion.So, kinematics is the branch of mechanics that deals with a body in motion without talking into account the cause of motion.

      Dynamics : The term 'Dynamics' is derived from the Greek word 'dynamics' meaning power.Dynemics is the branch of mechanics that deals with a body in motion taking into account the cause of motion.

    2. Physical quantity

      3. The quantity which is measurable by some physical means is known as physical quantity.Physical quantity obeys the physical laws.For example, mass, length, time, area, density and so on.All phycial quantities are measurable and they have both magnitude and unit.So, a physical quantities is expressed as;

      Physical quantity=magnitude * unit =numerical value * unit =N * U

      Fundamental Quantity : The basic physical quantity which is taken as standard to measure other physical quantities is known as fundamental quantity.In general, seven physical quantities are considered as fundamental quantities. They are length, mass and time (mechanics); Luminous intensity (optics); temperature (Heat); electric current (Electricity) and amount of chemical substance (Nuclear physics).In addition, there are two sub-fundamental quantities viz; plane angle and solid solid angle.

      Derived Quantity : A quantity obtained from fundamental quantities is called a derived quantity. Area, volume, density, speed, electric intensity, magnetic potential are some example of derived quantities.The derived quantities are expressed in terms of fundamental quantities.

    3. Units and Measurement

      4. The process of comparing an unknown quantity with a known standered refrence is called measurement in physics.The known standered reference used for comparison is called unit.For example, if the mass of a ball is 3 kg, it means that the unit of mass is the kilogram and this unit is contained 3 times in the mass of the ball.

      Fundamental units : A unit of a fundamental quantity is called a fundamental unit.For example, gram for mass;meter for length and so on.

      Derived unit : A unit of derived quantity is called a derived unit.For example,m/s for speed; gm/cm^3 for density and so on.

      6. The major limitations of dimensional formula are listed below:

      • It cannot say whether a physical quantity is a scalar or a vector.

  • Vector

    1. Scalar Quantity

      2. A physical quantity for which only the magnitude is sufficient enough to be completely descrived and need not be specified with direction is called scalar quantity or simple scalar.Examples: mass, time, volume, speed, distance, work, energy etc

    2. Vector Quantity

      3. A physical quantity for which both the magnitude and direction need to be compulsorily defined or apecified for its complete description is called vector quantity or simple vector.Example: displacement, velocity, momentum, force, impulse, acceleration, weight, current density, electric field, etc.

    3. Difference between Scalars and Vectors

      4. S.No. Scalars Vectors
      1 They posses only magnitude They posses both magnitude and direction.
      2 They obey the ordinary laws of algebra. They do not obey the ordinary laws of algebra.
      3 They change if magnitude changes. They change if either magnitude or direction or both changes.
      4 They are represented by ordinary letters. They are represented by bold-faced letter or letter having arrow over them.

    4. Kinds of a Vector

      5. Zero or null vector : A vector having zero magnitude is called a zero or null vector. Its initial and terminal points coincide. It has an arbitrary direction i.e., it can have any direction. The result of addition of a vector to its own negative vector results zero vector.

      Unit vector : A vector having unit magnitude is called a unit vector. It is represented by n cap.A unit vector is obtained by dividing a vector by its magnitude.

      Properties of a unit vector :
      • Magnitude of unit vector is unity i.e., for a unit vector n cap,modulus n cap=1.
      • It just gives the direction of a vector.
      • Unit vector has no units of dimensions.

    5. Parallelogram law of vector addition

      6. It state that if two vectors acting simultaneously at a point are represented both in magnitude and direction by two adjactent sides of a parallelogram drawn from a point, then the diagonal of the parallelogram passing through that point represents the resultant both in magnitude and direction.

      If vector A and B are represented by the sides of a parallelogram OPQS, and resultant vector R is represented by diagonal OQ such that resultant vector R = vector A + vectro

      Magnitude of vector R : To calculate the magnitude of the resultant vector R, let us draw a perpendicular at N from Q when OS is produced.Let the angle between vector A and vector B be 0.

      In triangle ONQ
      OQ2 = ON2 + NQ2
      OQ2 = (OS +SN)2 + NQ2
      sin A = NQ / QS = NQ / B
      NQ = Bsin A

    6. Triangle law of vector addition

      7. If the two sides of a triangle completely represent two vector both in magnitude and direction taken in same order, then the third side taken in opposite order represents the resultant of the two vectors both in magnitude and direction.

      Let there be two vector A and B acting on a particle simultaneously represented both magnitude and direction by the sides OP and PQ. Let A be the angle between them. Then, according to the triangle law of vectors, the side OQ represents their resultant vector R which makes an angle A with the vector A. So, the resultant vector is
      vector R = vector A + vector B

      Magnitude of vector R : Let us drop a perpendicular at N from Q where OP is produced to ON.
      In triangle ONQ
      OQ2 = ON2 + NQ2
      OQ2 = (OP + PN)2 + NQ2................eq(i)
      In triangle PNQ,
      cos A = PN / PQ = PN / B
      PN = Bcos A
      and sin A = QN / PQ = QN / B
      QN = Bsin A

      from equation (i)
      R2 = (A + Bcos A)2 + (Bsin A)2
      R2 = A2 + 2AB cos A + B2cos2A + B2sin2A
      R2 = A2 + 2AB cos A + B2
      R = root under A2 + 2AB cos A + B2

      which is the magnitude of the resultant vector.

      Direction of vector R : As the resultant vector R makes an angle Z with vector A, then in triangle ONQ,

      tan Z = QN / ON = QN / (OP+PN) = Bsin A / (A+Bcos A)
      Z = tan-1(Bsin A / A+Bcos A)

      This is required direction of the resultant vector.

    7. Laws of motion

      8. Friction:

      The retarding force which comes into play when one body moves in contact with another is called friction or frictional force.

      Causes of friction:

      According to classical theory, the surface of body contains large number of irregularities even if the surface is highly polished, when viewed by microscope.Thus the friction aries due to interlocking of irregularities of two surface in contact.
      According to modern theory, the force of friction is due to the force of adhesion or coheion between the molecules on the two surfaces at the point of actual contact.But actual cause of friction is still unknown.
